worldbench is a **human-as-judge benchmark**: one natural-language prompt,
several models, no rubric, no automated scorer. You look at what each model
built and decide for yourself whether it holds together.

## What it actually tests

Every model gets the same prompt and has to produce a single self-contained
Three.js scene (a floating, multi-biome voxel island) from a text
description alone, with no image, no schema, no reference file. Getting
that right takes more than scene styling. It forces the model to reason
about several different things at once:

- **Spatial / relational placement**: the prompt encodes a placement graph,
  not a list. Snow mountains sit behind the snowy conifer forest, which sits
  beside the highlands, which slope down into a flowering grove; desert and
  volcano have to stay physically and hydrologically separate. The model has
  to hold that whole adjacency graph in its head and lay out geometry that
  satisfies it, not just place ten biomes wherever they fit.
- **Ecological causality**: water has to behave like water. Mountain melt
  feeds a wet corridor through the forest, highlands, jungle, and grassland,
  then widens into a coastal delta. Desert is a rain-shadow basin with no
  through-river. The volcano sits outside that whole system, and lava
  touching water is supposed to turn into obsidian. None of this is
  decorative; it's a small causal system the model has to simulate
  correctly, not just describe.
- **Elevation and scale reasoning**: peaks dominate, shelves sit mid-height,
  plains stay low, and cliffs drop sharply, and every biome still has to
  read as its own region from a high orbit instead of collapsing into its
  neighbor.
- **Ecological grounding for flora and fauna**: cacti belong in the desert,
  pines in the snow forest, deer at jungle edges and across the grassland.
  Placing life that actually matches its climate is a grounding task, not
  decoration.
- **Temporal / systems reasoning**: a day/night cycle, a seasonal cycle,
  and cyclic weather (rain, snow, blizzard, sandstorm, ash) that has to stay
  staggered by biome instead of blanketing the whole island at once.
- **UI and interaction logic**: a clickable biome legend that flies the
  camera to each region, plus a live season readout, testing whether the
  model can wire 3D scene state to on-screen UI, not just render geometry.
- **Constrained code generation**: everything ships as one self-contained
  HTML file, no build step, no separate assets, CDN Three.js only. It has
  to just open in a browser and run.

There's no scoring because there's no single correct island, only a
hand-tuned reference built by a human, and your own eyes to compare it
against a range of very different automated attempts.

## The placement graph

This is the same wet corridor described in the prompt, just drawn as a
graph instead of prose. Follow the solid arrows and the required path is a
single line: mountain melt feeds the conifer forest, then the highlands,
then the jungle, then the grassland plateau, then out to the coastal
delta.

Two exceptions break that single line, and both are drawn differently on
purpose. The flowering grove has two valid sources instead of one: its
main feed comes down from the highlands, and the dashed teal "and/or" edge
shows the grassland plateau as an equally valid alternate. Separately, the
dashed amber edge into the volcano marks the one causal rule that still
reaches it: lava touching water quenches to obsidian, even though the
volcano gets no through-river. Desert and volcano stay dashed boxes
because the prompt keeps both deliberately cut off from the water system,
and the model has to preserve that isolation rather than route a river
through either one.

## Try it yourself

You don't need this site to run the benchmark, just the prompt and a
model.

1. Open the prompt below and copy it.
2. Paste it into any model chat: `Claude`, `ChatGPT`, `Gemini`, whatever
   you want to try.
3. Save the raw output, with no surrounding text, as `world.html`.
4. Open that file in your browser and look around.
